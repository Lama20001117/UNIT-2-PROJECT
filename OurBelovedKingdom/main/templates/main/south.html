The Southern Region

Before the Saudi rule, the conditions in the regions

Southern Saudi Arabia:

The situation in the southwest region of the country encompassed Asir, Jazan (Al-Makhlaf Al-Sulaimani), and Najran. The influence of the nobility extended from Al-Ula in the north to Al Qunfudhah in the south, covering the Tihama of Asir and its surroundings. Their influence was more pronounced in Tihama due to the ease of military movement along its coastal parts. In Sarat, some tribes were allied with the nobility, while others pledged allegiance to local leaders. The economic life in Asir relied on agriculture, fishing, and livestock, influenced by political circumstances and natural factors. Generally, economic conditions were not dire due to seasonal rainfall. Jazan, also known as Al-Makhlaf Al-Sulaimani, was named after Sulaiman bin Tariq Al-Hukmi, who unified the region in 373 Hijri (983 CE). His emirate ended with the rise of Hussein bin Salamah, transforming Al-Makhlaf into multiple emirates led by nobles until the mid-twelfth century Hijri, when Sharif Ahmed bin Mohammed bin Khayrat successfully unified the region under his leadership. Economic activities in Al-Makhlaf relied on agriculture, trade, livestock, and fishing, with active trade ports and fertile lands. In Najran, the majority of its inhabitants were from the Yam tribes, renowned for their military activity, which was a significant source of income. Often, the leaders of Al-Makhlaf Al-Sulaimani sought their assistance in wars. The Makkaramahs were the religious leaders of Najran until it came under the rule of the Al Sauds.
